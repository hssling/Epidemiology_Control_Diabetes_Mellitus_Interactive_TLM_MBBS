Definition & Diagnostic Criteria

Diabetes Mellitus (DM) is a group of metabolic disorders characterized by chronic hyperglycemia resulting from defects in insulin secretion, insulin action, or both.

WHO Definition

A metabolic disorder characterized by chronic hyperglycemia with disturbances of carbohydrate, fat and protein metabolism resulting from defects in insulin secretion, insulin action, or both.

Types of Diabetes Mellitus

Type 1 DM

  • Autoimmune Œ≤-cell destruction
  • Absolute insulin dependence
  • Peak incidence: 10-14 years
  • Global: 10-15% of cases
  • India: 5-10% of cases

Type 2 DM

  • Insulin resistance + Œ≤-cell failure
  • Gradual onset, often asymptomatic
  • Peak incidence: 45-65 years
  • Global: 85-90% of cases
  • India: 90-95% of cases

Gestational DM

  • Glucose intolerance during pregnancy
  • Screen at 24-28 weeks
  • Maternal & fetal risks
  • Revert postpartum in most
  • High T2DM risk later

Other Specific Types

  • MODY: Genetic, young onset
  • Mitochondrial: Maternal inheritance
  • Pancreatic: Disease or resection
  • Drug-induced: Steroids, diuretics
  • Genetic syndromes: Down, Klinefelter

Risk Factors & Etiology

Genetic Factors

  • T1DM: HLA-DR3/DR4 (50-60%)
  • T2DM: TCF7L2, PPARG, KCNJ11
  • Family history: 3-4x increased risk
  • South Asian phenotype

Lifestyle Factors

  • Sedentary lifestyle: 1.4x risk
  • Unhealthy diet: High GI, processed foods
  • Smoking: 20-50% increased risk
  • Alcohol: Binge > moderate

Metabolic Factors

  • Obesity: BMI >25, central adiposity
  • Dyslipidemia: Low HDL, High TG
  • Hypertension: >130/80 mmHg
  • Metabolic syndrome

Other Factors

  • Age: Risk doubles per decade
  • GDM: 40-60% develop DM later
  • Polycystic ovary syndrome
  • Socioeconomic status

Risk Assessment Tools

Indian Diabetes Risk Score (IDRS)

  • 4 parameters: Age, abdominal obesity, family history, physical activity
  • Score range: 0-20
  • Risk categories: Low (<30), Medium (30-50), High (>50)
  • Sensitivity: 70-80%

FINDRISC Score

  • 8 parameters including age, BMI, medications
  • Score ‚â•14: High risk (one-third will develop diabetes)
  • Validates across ethnic groups

Clinical Features & Complications

Classic Symptoms (3 P's)

  • Polyuria: >3L urine/day, osmotic diuresis
  • Polydipsia: Excessive thirst, dehydration
  • Polyphagia: Hunger despite eating
  • Fatigue: Reduced energy metabolism
  • Weight loss: Especially in T1DM
  • Blurred vision: Fluctuating hyperglycemia

Physical Signs

Skin Changes

  • Acanthosis nigricans
  • Diabetic dermopathy
  • Fungal infections

Oral Signs

  • Periodontal disease
  • Candidiasis
  • Xerostomia

Foot Changes

  • Neuropathy
  • Vascular insufficiency
  • Charcot's arthropathy

First-Line Therapy

Metformin

  • Mechanism: ‚Üì Hepatic glucose production, ‚Üë peripheral uptake
  • Dose: 500-1000 mg twice daily with meals
  • Benefits: Weight neutral, CV protection, inexpensive
  • Side effects: GI upset, B12 deficiency
  • Contraindications: eGFR <30, liver disease

Second-Line Additions

GLP-1 Receptor Agonists

  • Examples: Dulaglutide (weekly), Semaglutide (oral/weekly)
  • Benefits: Weight loss, CV protection, low hypoglycemia
  • Use: High-risk patients, weight management
  • Administration: Subcutaneous injection

SGLT2 Inhibitors

  • Empagliflozin, Dapagliflozin, Canagliflozin
  • Renal glucose excretion mechanism
  • CV & renal protection proven
  • Weight loss: 2-3 kg
  • Side effects: Genital infections, DKA

DPP-4 Inhibitors

  • Sitagliptin, Vildagliptin, Teneligliptin
  • Glucose-dependent insulin secretion
  • Weight neutral, low hypoglycemia risk
  • Renal dose adjustment needed
  • No CV benefit proven

Sulfonylureas

  • Glimepiride, Glipizide, Gliclazide
  • Insulin secretion stimulation
  • Rapid onset, inexpensive
  • Hypoglycemia risk, weight gain
  • Short-acting preferred (gliclazide)

Insulin Therapy

  • T1DM: Basal-bolus regimen
  • T2DM: Add to OHA failure
  • Basal: Degludec/Lantus (once daily)
  • Bolus: Aspart/Lispro pre-meal
  • Education essential before starting

Prevention Strategies

Levels of Prevention

Primary Prevention

Prevent onset of diabetes in high-risk individuals

Secondary Prevention

Prevent progression of prediabetes to diabetes

Tertiary Prevention

Prevent complications and disability

Dietary Prevention

Key Principles

  • Calorie control: 500 kcal deficit for weight loss
  • Glycemic load: Low GI foods preferred
  • Carbohydrates: 45-60% of calories
  • Fiber intake: 25-30g/day
  • Fat composition: <30% total, <10% saturated

Indian Context

  • Rice-based South ‚Üí Whole grains
  • Wheat-based North ‚Üí Whole wheat
  • Festival foods ‚Üí Healthy modifications
  • Cultural preferences ‚Üí Plant-based proteins
