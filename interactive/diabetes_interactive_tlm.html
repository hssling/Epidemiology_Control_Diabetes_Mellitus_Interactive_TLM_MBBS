Type 1 DM: Autoimmune Destruction

Pathogenesis of Type 1 Diabetes involves an autoimmune destruction of pancreatic β-cells, leading to absolute insulin deficiency. This process begins with genetic predisposition and environmental triggers that initiate an immune response against insulin-producing cells.

Pathogenesis of Type 1 Diabetes
Genetic Predisposition (HLA-DR3/DR4) ↓ Environmental Trigger (Viral infection) ↓ Autoimmune Attack • ICA, GADA, IA-2A antibodies present • Lymphocyte infiltration of islets ↓ β-Cell Destruction • Progressive loss of insulin secretion • First-phase insulin response lost ↓ Absolute Insulin Deficiency • Complete dependence on exogenous insulin • Lifelong insulin replacement required
Characteristic Features:
  • Autoantibodies present in 85-90%
  • C-peptide levels low/undetectable
  • Acute onset, often in childhood
  • Ketoacidosis common at presentation

Type 2 DM: Insulin Resistance + β-Cell Failure

Pathogenesis of Type 2 Diabetes involves progressive insulin resistance combined with gradual β-cell failure. This complex interplay between genetic factors, lifestyle choices, and cellular dysfunction leads to impaired glucose homeostasis and eventual hyperglycemia.

Pathogenesis of Type 2 Diabetes
Genetic Predisposition + Lifestyle Factors ↓ Obesity ↓ Insulin Resistance • Impaired glucose uptake • Increased hepatic glucose output • Compensatory hyperinsulinemia ↓ Progressive β-Cell Failure • Glucotoxicity • Lipotoxicity • Apoptosis ↓ Relative Insulin Deficiency • Inadequate insulin for needs • Post-prandial hyperglycemia
Characteristic Features:
  • Insulin resistance primary defect
  • β-cell failure progressive
  • Strong genetic component
  • Lifestyle factors critical

Hypothalamic Regulation

  • Autonomic nervous system: Sympathetic/parasympathetic balance
  • HPA axis: Stress response and cortisol regulation

Type 2 Diabetes Mellitus

Characteristics of Type 2 DM

Specific Types Due to Other Causes

  • MODY-2 (Glucokinase): Mild, responsive to lifestyle
  • MODY-3 (HNF1A): Most common, sulfonylurea responsive
  • MODY-1 (HNF4A): Rare, macrosomia in offspring
  • MODY-5 (HNF1B): Associated with renal cysts

Definition & Criteria

Understanding Diabetes Mellitus

Comprehensive overview of diabetes definition, historical perspectives, and classification systems

What is Diabetes Mellitus?

WHO Definition (2019)

"A metabolic disorder characterized by chronic hyperglycemia with disturbances of carbohydrate, fat and protein metabolism resulting from defects in insulin secretion, insulin action, or both."

ADA Definition

"Diabetes mellitus is a group of diseases characterized by high blood glucose levels that result from defects in the body's ability to produce and/or use insulin."

Key Points:
  • Chronic hyperglycemia is the hallmark feature
  • Defects in insulin secretion or action are the underlying causes
  • Disturbances in carbohydrate, fat, and protein metabolism result
  • Long-term complications affect multiple organ systems

Historical Perspective

Ancient Times (400 BCE - 200 CE)

  • Ancient Indian texts describe "Madhumeha" (honey urine)
  • Greek physicians noticed sweet taste of urine
  • Chinese texts described wasting and thirst syndrome

19th Century

  • 1824: Identified as distinct clinical entity
  • 1869: Glucose identified in diabetic urine
  • Link between pancreas and diabetes established

20th Century Breakthroughs

  • 1921: Insulin discovery by Banting & Best
  • 1950s: Oral hypoglycemics introduced
  • 1970s: Home blood glucose monitoring
  • 1980s: HbA1c as diagnostic tool

Modern Era (2000s)

  • Incretins: GLP-1 agonists, DPP-4 inhibitors
  • SGLT2 inhibitors: Novel mechanism drugs
  • Continuous glucose monitoring: Advanced technology
  • Artificial pancreas: Closed-loop systems

Metabolic Impact of Diabetes

Carbohydrate Metabolism

  • Glycemia: Impaired glucose uptake and utilization
  • Glycogenolysis: Enhanced hepatic glucose output
  • Gluconeogenesis: Increased in uncontrolled diabetes
  • Glycosuria: Glucose spillage when >180 mg/dL

Lipid Metabolism

  • Lipolysis: Increased free fatty acid release
  • Ketogenesis: Acetyl-CoA converted to ketones
  • Dyslipidemia: Elevated triglycerides, LDL
  • Atherosclerosis: Accelerated vascular disease

Protein Metabolism

  • Protein catabolism: Increased muscle protein breakdown
  • Nitrogen loss: Negative nitrogen balance
  • Muscle wasting: Especially in uncontrolled Type 1 DM
  • Healing impairment: Delayed wound healing

Who Should Be Screened?

Universal Screening (ADA 2024)

  • Age ≥35 years: All adults
  • Overweight + risk factor: BMI ≥25 kg/m²
  • Family history: First-degree relative
  • High-risk ethnicity: South Asian, African American
  • Pregnancy: Universal GDM screening

Clinical Diagnosis

Symptoms + Random Glucose ≥200 mg/dL

Symptoms include:

  • Polyuria (>3L urine/day)
  • Polydipsia (excessive thirst)
  • Unexplained weight loss
  • Blurred vision
  • Recurrent infections

Single abnormal test result in symptomatic patient = Diabetes diagnosis

Special Diagnostic Considerations

Pediatric Cases

  • Same criteria as adults
  • Autoantibodies for T1DM
  • OGTT preferred in children
  • Consider monogenic forms

Pregnancy

  • GDM: Glucose intolerance first recognized in pregnancy
  • Screening: 24-28 weeks
  • 75g OGTT with specific cutoffs
  • Higher risk of T2DM postpartum

Elderly Patients

  • HbA1c may be unreliable
  • FPG/OGTT preferred
  • Liberalized targets
  • Screen for complications

T1DM: Basal-Bolus Regimen
  • Basal insulin (40-60%): Once daily, covers fasting
  • Bolus insulin (40-60%): Pre-meal, covers CHO intake
  • Insulin-to-carbohydrate ratio: Individualized
  • Correction factor: 1 unit reduces glucose by 50 mg/dL

Special Populations Management

Pregnancy & GDM

  • Pre-conception: HbA1c <6.5%, folate
  • GDM: Diet first, insulin if needed
  • Tight control: Fasting 95, postprandial 140
  • Post-delivery: Screen for T2DM at 6-12 weeks

Elderly Patients

  • Liberalized targets: HbA1c 7.5-8.5%
  • Avoid hypoglycemia: Short-acting agents
  • Minimize polypharmacy: Fixed-dose combos
  • Functional assessment: Comorbidities

Cardiovascular Disease

  • SGLT2 inhibitors preferred: Empagliflozin proven
  • Avoid sulfonylureas: Higher hypo risk
  • Metformin: Preferred first-line
  • GLP-1 agonists: Some have CV indication

Acute Complications Management

Hypoglycemia Emergency

15-15 Rule:
  1. 15g rapid glucose (juice, glucose tabs)
  2. Wait 15 minutes, recheck glucose
  3. Repeat if needed, then eat meal
  4. Severe cases: Glucagon 1mg IM or IV dextrose

DKA Management

Emergency Protocol:
  • Fluids: 1L NS/hour initially
  • Insulin: 0.14 U/kg/hour infusion
  • K+ correction: Monitor closely
  • Monitoring: Hourly glucose, q2h electrolytes

Diabetes in India

State-wise Prevalence in India shows significant regional variation, reflecting differences in lifestyle, urbanization, and socio-economic factors. Southern states like Kerala show highest prevalence due to increased life expectancy, urbanization, and traditional diets high in refined carbohydrates.

Primary Prevention

Diabetes Prevention Program (DPP) Model

DPP Results:
  • 58% reduction in diabetes incidence
  • Sustained lifestyle changes
  • Cost-effective long-term benefits
Core Components:
  • Weight loss: 5-7% body weight
  • Calorie deficit: 500-1000 kcal/day
  • Activity: 150 min/week moderate
  • Maintenance: 3+ years support
Intensive Lifestyle Intervention:
  • Individualized counseling sessions
  • Group lifestyle coaching
  • Telephone support (cost-effective)
  • Behavioral goal setting

Physical Activity & Exercise

Aerobic Activity

  • Duration: 150 minutes/week moderate
  • Intensity: 40-60% VO2 max
  • Frequency: Minimum 3 days/week
  • Types: Walking, cycling, swimming

Resistance Training

  • Frequency: 2-3 sessions/week
  • Muscle groups: Major groups involved
  • Progressive: Increase resistance over time
  • Sets/reps: 8-12 reps, 2-3 sets

Special Considerations

  • Yoga: Improved insulin sensitivity
  • Tai Chi: Balance and relaxation
  • Workplace: Walking breaks, standing desks
  • Home: Chores as activity

Diet & Nutritional Prevention

Dietary Patterns

Mediterranean Diet
  • Vegetables, fruits, whole grains
  • Fish, olive oil emphasis
  • 30-50% diabetes reduction
  • Anti-inflammatory effects
DASH Diet
  • Sodium <2.4g/day
  • Calcium and potassium rich
  • Reduced processed foods
  • Blood pressure control
Low Carbohydrate
  • 40-80g carbohydrates daily
  • Plant proteins emphasized
  • Superior weight loss
  • Sustainability challenged

Specific Modifications

Carbohydrate Quality
  • Low glycemic index/load
  • Whole grains vs refined
  • Fiber intake 25-30g/day
  • Complex carbohydrates
Fat Composition
  • Saturated fat <7% calories
  • PUFAs, omega-3 sources
  • MUFAs: olive oil, nuts
  • Trans fat avoidance
