# Risk Factors and Etiology of Diabetes Mellitus

## Classification of Risk Factors

### Modifiable Risk Factors
- **Lifestyle factors**: Diet, physical activity, smoking
- **Clinical factors**: Obesity, hypertension, dyslipidemia
- **Behavioral factors**: Stress, sleep patterns, medication use

### Non-Modifiable Risk Factors
- **Genetic factors**: Family history, ethnicity, genetic variants
- **Age**: Increasing risk with age
- **Gestational factors**: Previous GDM, fetal macrosomia
- **Demographic factors**: Gender, socioeconomic status

### Environmental Factors
- **Urbanization**: Lifestyle changes, reduced activity
- **Dietary shifts**: Processed foods, sugary drinks
- **Pollutants**: Endocrine disruptors
- **Infectious agents**: Viral triggers for T1DM

## Genetic Risk Factors

### Type 1 Diabetes Genetics
- **HLA genes**: DR3/DR4 haplotypes (major histocompatibility complex)
- **Other genes**: INS (insulin gene), PTPN22, CTLA4, IFIH1
- **Inheritance pattern**: Polygenic, multifactorial
- **Twin concordance**: ~50% in monozygotic twins vs 10-15% dizygotic
- **Heritability**: ~50-60% attributable to genetics

### Type 2 Diabetes Genetics
- **T allele of TCF7L2**: Strongest genetic risk factor (odds ratio ~1.4)
- **PPARG**: Peroxisome proliferator-activated receptor gamma
- **KCNJ11 and ABCC8**: Potassium channels in β-cells
- **FTO**: Fat mass and obesity-associated gene
- **TCF7L2**: Wnt signaling pathway
- **Multiple loci**: >400 SNPs associated with T2DM

### South Asian Genetic Susceptibility
- **Asian Indian phenotype**:
  - Increased visceral adiposity
  - Higher insulin resistance at lower BMI
  - Rapid β-cell failure
  - Strong genetic predisposition

- **Haplotype analysis**: South Asians have unique genetic variants
- **Gene-environment interaction**: Traditional lifestyle protective despite genes

## Demographic and Social Risk Factors

### Age and Gender
- **Peak incidence**: T2DM peaks at 45-65 years, T1DM at 10-14 years
- **Gender differences**:
  - T1DM: Slight male predominance in adults
  - T2DM: Higher risk in women during child-bearing years
  - Inverse in Asian Indians: Males have higher risk than females

### Socioeconomic Status
- **Urban vs rural**: Urban > rural (urban OR = 3.0)
- **Education level**: Illiterate > literate (OR = 1.5-2.5)
- **Occupation**: Sedentary > manual labor
- **Income**: Higher income groups at higher risk
- **Healthcare access**: Limited access increases complications

### Ethnic/Racial Variants
- **South Asians**: Highest risk worldwide (Brown phenotype)
- **Native Americans/Pacific Islanders**: Very high prevalence
- **African Americans**: 2-4 times higher risk than Caucasians
- **Hispanic Americans**: 2 times higher risk
- **Asian Americans**: Variable but higher than Caucasians

## Lifestyle and Behavioral Risk Factors

### Dietary Factors
- **High glycemic load**: Refined carbohydrates, sugary drinks
- **Low dietary fiber**: <25g/day associated with higher risk
- **High saturated fats**: Processed meats, fried foods
- **Low omega-3 fatty acids**: Protective effect diminished
- **Excessive calories**: Portion size and calorie density

### Physical Inactivity
- **Sedentary lifestyle**: TV time >2 hours/day (OR = 1.4)
- **Lack of exercise**: No regular physical activity
- **Occupational activity**: Desk jobs vs manual labor
- **Transportation**: Motorized vs walking/cycling

### Obesity and Body Composition
- **Overall obesity**: BMI >25 kg/m² risk increases
- **Central obesity**: Waist circumference key predictor
- **Asian Indian cutoffs**: Waist >90cm (males), >80cm (females)
- **Body fat distribution**: Visceral > subcutaneous fat
- **Obesity trajectory**: Weight gain in adulthood critical

### Smoking and Alcohol
- **Active smoking**: Increases T2DM risk by 20-50%
- **Passive smoking**: 20-30% increased risk
- **Alcohol**: Moderate consumption may be protective, excessive harmful
- **Alcohol patterns**: Binge drinking particularly harmful

## Metabolic Risk Factors

### Insulin Resistance Syndrome Components
- **Hypertension**: Systolic >130 mmHg associated with T2DM
- **Dyslipidemia**: Low HDL, high triglycerides
- **Obesity**: Central adiposity
- **Impaired glucose tolerance**: Precursor state
- **High ALT/AST**: Liver fat accumulation

### Prediabetes as Risk Factor
- **Natural progression rate**: 5-10% per year to diabetes
- **Intervention window**: Chance for primary prevention
- **IGT vs IFG**: IGT carries higher risk of progression
- **Regression possible**: Lifestyle changes can reverse

## Environmental and Occupational Factors

### Urbanization and Modernization
- **Built environment**: Limited parks, unsafe streets
- **Food environment**: Easy access to fast food, limited healthy options
- **Work environment**: Sedentary jobs, shift work
- **Social environment**: Changed family structures, reduced community support

### Microbiome and Infections
- **Gut microbiome**: Altered in obesity and T2DM
- **Viral infections**: Coxsackie B, Rubella may trigger T1DM
- **Gut permeability**: "Leaky gut" hypothesis in T1DM
- **Antibiotic exposure**: May alter microbiome development

### Endocrine Disruptors
- **Persistent organic pollutants**: Dioxins, PCB, pesticides
- **Bisphenol-A (BPA)**: Used in plastics, associated with obesity
- **Phthalates**: Present in cosmetics and plastics
- **Mechanisms**: Affect hormonal signaling, insulin sensitivity

## Maternal and Fetal Factors

### Gestational Diabetes
- **Previous GDM**: 40-60% develop DM within 10 years
- **Offspring risk**: 2-3 fold higher risk of obesity and DM
- **Epigenetic effects**: Fetal programming changes

### Intrauterine Environment
- **Fetal macrosomia**: Indicates maternal hyperglycemia
- **Low birth weight**: Associated with later insulin resistance
- **Maternal nutrition**: Poor maternal nutrition programs for adult DM
- **Catch-up growth**: Rapid postnatal growth increases risk

## Medical and Pharmacological Factors

### Medications Associated with Diabetes
- **Glucocorticoids**: Prednisone, dexamethasone, budesonide
- **Antipsychotics**: Olanzapine, risperidone (atypical)
- **β-blockers**: Non-selective agents
- **Thiazide diuretics**: Higher doses in equivalent
- **Statins**: Controversial, weak effect
- **Protease inhibitors**: HIV therapy

### Medical Conditions
- **Polycystic ovary syndrome**: 50-70% have insulin resistance
- **Cushing's syndrome**: Glucocorticoid excess
- **Acromegaly**: Growth hormone excess
- **Pheochromocytoma**: Catecholamine excess
- **Hemochromatosis**: Iron overload
- **Chronic pancreatitis**: Pancreatic destruction

## Emerging Risk Factors

### Sleep Disorders
- **Obstructive sleep apnea**: BMI-independent risk factor
- **Short sleep duration**: <6 hours increases risk
- **Shift work**: Disrupts circadian rhythms
- **Insomnia**: Chronic stress on glucose metabolism

### Mental Health Factors
- **Depression**: Bidirectional relationship with T2DM
- **Stress**: Cortisol-mediated insulin resistance
- **Antidepressants**: Some increase metabolic risk
- **Eating disorders**: Particularly binge eating

### Vitamin D Deficiency
- **Prevalence**: High in South Asians (50-80%)
- **Mechanism**: Impaired insulin secretion and sensitivity
- **Epidemiology**: Associated with T2DM in multiple studies
- **Intervention**: May be protective at adequate levels

### Air Pollution
- **Particulate matter**: PM2.5 associated with T2DM
- **Traffic-related pollution**: NOx exposure
- **Mechanism**: Inflammation and oxidative stress
- **Epidemiology**: Studies from major Indian cities show association

## Risk Factor Interactions

### Clustering of Risk Factors
- **Metabolic syndrome**: Multiple risk factors cluster
- **Gene-environment interaction**: Same environment, different genetic response
- **Epigenetics**: Early life environment modifies gene expression

### Population Attributable Risk
- **Obesity**: 80-85% of T2DM cases attributable to overweight/obesity
- **Physical inactivity**: 7% PAR (population attributable risk)
- **Dietary factors**: 15-20% combinations of diet and activity
- **Age**: Increasing due to population aging

## Risk Assessment Tools

### Indian Diabetes Risk Score (IDRS)
- **Components**: Age, abdominal obesity, family history, physical activity
- **Scoring system**: 0-20 points
- **Risk categories**: Low (<30), Medium (30-50), High (>50)
- **Predictive value**: Sensitivity 70-80%

### Finnish Diabetes Risk Score (FINDRISC)
- **Components**: Age, BMI, waist, physical activity, fruit/vegetables, blood pressure medication, hyperglycemia history
- **Score range**: 0-26 points
- **Risk stratification**: Low, moderate, high, very high risk groups

## Prevention Strategies Based on Risk Factors

### Primary Prevention
- **High-risk groups**: IDRS >50 or FINDRISC >14
- **Lifestyle intervention**: Diet + exercise (moderate to vigorous)
- **Pharmacological**: Metformin in high-risk individuals
- **Population strategy**: Policy changes for healthier environment

### Secondary Prevention
- **Prediabetes management**: Lifestyle + metformin
- **Regression of risk**: Weight loss, increased activity
- **Progression prevention**: Monitoring and early intervention

## Learning Objectives
- Identify major risk factors for diabetes
- Understand gene-environment interactions
- Assess individual risk using screening tools
- Apply prevention strategies based on risk assessment
- Recognize emerging and modifiable risk factors
- Understand population-level risk factor control
